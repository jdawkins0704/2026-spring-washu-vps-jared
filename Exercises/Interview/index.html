Interview Feature

Where design meets movement

A conversation on the intersection of architecture and track and field through the perspective of Olivia, an All-American High Jumper.

Read the Interview

About the Interview

Architecture, athletics, and adaptability

This interview explores how Olivia’s experience as an architecture major shapes the way she thinks about movement, form, structure, and critique in track and field. Through her reflections, it becomes clear that both design and athletics rely on precision, adaptability, and a deep understanding of how small decisions can influence larger outcomes.

Interview

Questions and Responses

01

How does your background in architecture influence the way you approach your events, especially something as technical as the high jump?

Having a background in design makes Olivia want to understand how things work and study the details behind them. Rather than focusing only on the end result, she pays attention to the mechanics, movement, and form of the high jump. That mindset helps her better understand how her body moves and what she needs to do to clear the bar.

02

In design, small changes can completely impact the final structure. Do you see a similar effect with small adjustments in your technique during jumps?

Yes. Olivia sees a strong parallel between architecture and track because even a small change can shape the final outcome. In jumping, one adjustment in the approach can affect everything before the athlete even leaves the ground. In architecture, one decision made early in the design process can change the entire direction of a project by the time of final review.

03

Has being an athlete changed the way you think about designing spaces, especially in terms of movement, flow, or human experience?

Being an athlete has given Olivia a stronger sense of community, which influences the way she thinks about design. She is more aware of how people move through spaces and how environments can shape experiences. When she looks at sports facilities, for example, she notices details like how light enters the building and how people naturally flow throughout the space.

04

Do you think being in a creative field like architecture gives you an advantage in a sport that’s often seen as purely physical?

Olivia believes that being in design school gives her a different understanding of life and change. Architecture teaches her that she can always adjust, revise, and take control of a situation, and that same adaptability shows up in track and field. It helps her respond to setbacks, make quick adjustments, and keep moving forward.

05

How does critique function in both spaces, design critiques in architecture versus coaching feedback in track, and how have you learned to respond to both?

Olivia sees a key difference between the two. In architecture, critique is usually focused on the work and the final product. In track, critique is more personal because it focuses on the body and how it moves. If someone dislikes a wall in a design, that wall can be changed. In track, the feedback is about changing something within yourself. She also points out that architecture is often more subjective, while track is much more objective.

06

Are there any design principles you’ve learned in architecture that you consciously or subconsciously apply to your training or technique?

One major lesson Olivia has carried from architecture into athletics is how to accept critique and keep going. In architecture, people regularly respond to your work after hours of effort, and over time that teaches resilience. Because of that, if she performs badly or someone is disappointed, she is better able to process it, pivot, and continue competing.

Takeaways

What this intersection reveals

Precision matters

In both architecture and track, small decisions can have a major impact on the final result.

Movement shapes experience

Athletic experience gives Olivia a stronger awareness of how people interact with and move through physical spaces.

Critique builds resilience

Both design and sport demand the ability to receive feedback, adapt, and keep improving.
